Identify the scattering in shallow Homestake structure.

An array of broadband seismometers with 3D geometry has been deployed at the former Homestake Mine in South Dakota to study the underground seismic field and noise. This data has provided us a good chance to understand how wavefield varies with depth and to improve teleseismic earthquake signals.

To analyze the influence of near-surface scattering on teleseismic signals, fk analysis has been applied to a teleseismic earthquake in Japan. We compare the result of fk analysis at different frequency ranges, in different depths, and show that (1) the "deep" stations (4000's) have a more coherent signal than the "shallow" stations (0-300), (2) station geometry have an influence on the shape of the fk peak, but roughness of the fk plot is due to the data, (3) higher frequency range we stack, the incoherent the fk plot looks, (4) limitation of the station number and distribution makes it hard to identify how the incoherence varies with space. This result seems to be consistent with near-surface scattering and potentially indicates that the scattering complexity decreases with depth.

Why Homestake?

Complex geology has made it a ideal place to study near-surface scattering. The cause of this project is, to some extent, related to the LIGO Project, aiming to examine the potential site for next generation of LIGO.

Why analyze the shallow shallow scattering?

Currently, most geophysical observation are based on the data recorded on surface station. We believe the shallow structure could cause a big variance on the distant signals, especially at geologically complex area. It's our goal to identify the influence of shallow structure and to find ways to correct the noisy signals.

Active Source Experiment in Homestake Mine

To analyze the influence of velocity structure on wavefield, it is extremely important to obtain the very near-surface structure because it would provide vital and accurate restraints on the deeper research. These motivated our design of an active source survey. We use the weight-drop hammer as the source and record the seismic signals via a land streamline device. With the active source data, we invert for both P wave and S wave velocity structure: P wave inversion with wavepath eikonal traveltime method; S wave inversion with a joint-inversion method for Rayleigh-wave dispersion and horizontal-to-vertical (H/V) spectral ratios.

For more details, see my Progress Report I

This work is corporated with Prof. Lee Liberty and Gabe Gribler in Boise State University.

Analyze Active Source Data:

For P wave inversion

A standard refraction processing flow would be undertaken to develop the P wave structure from the first shock arrival time. The method is based on wavepath eikonal traveltime inversion, which computes wavepaths by using finite-difference solutions to the eikonal equation.

Imaging the Earth's Core–Mantle Boundary with Seismic Migration

The bottom 200 – 400 km of the mantle (also referred to as D'' region or the lowermost mantle) represents one of the most dramatic thermal and compositional layers within our planet. Despite the region’s importance for understanding processes such as mantle convection and mixing, the distribution of chemical heterogeneity in the mantle and core-mantle coupling remains poorly understood.


A particularly enigmatic feature of this region is the intermittent presence of the D'' discontinuity, which marks the upper surface of the D'' region in some areas. Possible causes for the D'' discontinuity include thermal or compositional gradients. Although recent results from mineral physics have also suggested a phase change in perovskite as candidate mechanism.

We would like to apply a seismic migration/inversion technique to a data set of long-period waveforms in order to image structure in the D'' region. The phase stripped traces were converted to zero phase by subtracting the phase spectra from the reference pulse, and used as the data for a weighted migration based on the inverse scattering approach (the Generalised Radon Transform, GRT).

For more infomation, see related papers: van_der_Hilst2007, P. Wang2006a, P. Wang2006b, Chambers2006

Seismic Data Regularization

Because of acquisition cost or environmental factors, seismic data is usually incomplete which would affect following data processing. To solve this problem, data reconstruction has been widely studied. Some methods based on composed sensing has been developed in recent years, such as Curvelet Recovery by Sparsity-promoting Inversion method(CRSI).

CRSI reconstructs randomly lacked seismic data to get a high-SNR data, taking advantage of seismic waveform's sparse representation in the Curvelet domain based on the steepest descent algorithm that ensures accuracy and stability of the iteration.

Also, we want to combine it with compresive sensing and K-SVD dictionary learning. So that the over complete dictionary could adaptively feature through the training samples, and adaptively select transform basis acording to the characteristics of procesed data itself. The over complete dictionary provides a flexible way to seismic data sparse extension,and could lead to a beter reconstruction result.

For more infomation, see related papers: Curvelet_domain(in Chinese), Dreamlet_domain(in Chinese), 5D interpolation
